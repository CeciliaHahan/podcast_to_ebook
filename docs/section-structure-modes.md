# Section Structure Modes

This project now uses two section-level content shapes at the `Draft` layer.

The goal is simple:
- do not force every section into an analytical frame
- do not lose analytical clarity when a section is genuinely argument-heavy

## 1. Argument-Driven Sections

Definition:
- the section mainly works by making a judgment and then supporting it
- readers benefit from seeing why the judgment holds, and where its limits are

Typical signals:
- clear judgment density
- support relationships are explicit
- the section contains boundaries, caveats, or corrective framing
- the value is mainly in reasoning, not just in atmosphere or character texture

Preferred rendering:
- `主要观点`
- `为什么这么说`
- `但也要看到` (optional)

Typical samples:
- international affairs analysis
- strategy or business analysis
- sections of culture commentary that clearly move through claim -> support -> correction

## 2. Situational Sections

Definition:
- the section mainly works through scenes, anecdotes, reactions, interpersonal dynamics, or character texture
- readers benefit more from concrete examples than from analytical reframing

Typical signals:
- the value comes from what happened, who said what, and how people reacted
- support is better preserved as examples or scenes
- the section feels weaker when over-compressed into abstract reasoning

Preferred rendering:
- `主要观点`
- `主要论据与例子`

Typical samples:
- personality-heavy interviews
- story-driven talk sections
- roundtable sections whose value is in relationship, vibe, or scene details

## Why This Is Section-Level

One episode can contain both shapes.

Example:
- one section may be strongly analytical
- the next section may mainly work as a scene, anecdote, or character portrait

So the project should not classify the whole episode once and force one structure onto every section.

## Practical Rule

Use `argument-driven` when the section is primarily:
- claim
- support
- boundary/correction

Use `situational` when the section is primarily:
- scene
- reaction
- anecdote
- relationship

This keeps the material readable without flattening the podcast's original texture.
